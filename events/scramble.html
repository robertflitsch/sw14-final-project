Scramble for Africa

Between 1881 and the beginning of World War I, European nations pushed into Africa in competition for land and resources. The movement began with Belgium's occupation of the Congo and the British involvement in the construction of the Suez Canal and then took off rapidly, each European state struggling to stake his claim. In a true display of "New Imperialism," the countries began to impose more direct control of their conquered nations with imported government and settlers.

This veritable stampede for land was legitimized with the Berlin Conference when the European powers got together to carve up the continent. Though most of the Western powers were represented in the conference, not a single African diplomat was present. By the end of the process, virtually every inch of Africa (with the exception of Liberia and Ethiopia) was in the hands of a foreign power.

Because of the moral implications of the New Imperialist movement, the British and other European governments taking over the continent felt themselves in the right. They were on a grand mission to civilize this far corner of the world. Though some backlash by authors and politicians such as Joseph Conrad did follow the process, powerful figures like Cecil Rhodes and Joseph Chamberlain dominated the public opinion and held Britain largely in the right.
