Sepoy Rebellion

When British officials presented Hindu and Muslim soldiers with rifle cartridges greased by cow and pig fat, rebellion ensued. Sometimes known as the "First War of Independence" to the Indians, The Sepoy Rebellion of 1857 was a watershed point in British imperial history.

Prior to the mutiny, India was a divided nation, under the sovereignty of various monarchies and empires, including the once powerful Mughal Empire. The British East India Company presided over the domain, its involvement differing from region to region. The company retained its control with standing armies comprised of native "sepoys" under the rule of British officers. In the wake of the Battle of Plessy in 1757, the British began a policy of slightly more direct rule, annexing territories as rulers were deemed "incompetent" and sending armies to new lands. The rebellion was sparked when a new musket was distributed to the troops. This new weapon needed to be loaded manually so its cartridges were greased to make the process faster. With the religious ramifications of cow and pig fat unbeknownst to the British officials, the soldiers were ordered to load their weapons. The outraged sepoys laid siege but were soon crushed by British troops. After the mutiny, control of India moved from the private to public sphere. The crown took control of the land, making Queen Victoria the Empress of India. The results of the Sepoy Rebellion reflected a larger trend of the Empire. The " Empire of Liberty" was moving towards a stronger domain. This rebellion planted the seeds of New Imperialism.
